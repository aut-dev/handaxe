# Location Design: TODD'S APARTMENT

## A Day in the Life of Todd — Handaxe Studios

---

## Overview

A studio apartment in a mid-range building in a mid-sized American city. Not a dump, not nice. The kind of place you find on Craigslist in an afternoon when you've never apartment-hunted before and you just need somewhere to be. Todd has lived here for three months. It looks like he's lived here for three days.

**Address/neighborhood:** Never specified. We never see the exterior. We never see the hallway. Todd's apartment is the entire world of the show.

## THE KITCHEN

The kitchen is the show. 90% of screen time happens here. Every detail matters.

### Layout
- **Galley-style** along the back wall, roughly 8 feet long
- Open to the main room — no dividing wall, just a counter edge where the kitchen ends and the living space begins
- The counter where Todd props his phone sits at the **end of the galley**, perpendicular to the back wall, creating an L-shape. This is the camera position for most of the show

### The Counter (Camera Position)
- Laminate countertop, faux-butcher-block pattern. Not ugly, just cheap
- This is where Todd's phone lives during filming. He props it against whatever's nearby — a cereal box, a stack of cans, a mug. The prop changes episode to episode (subtle continuity detail)
- From this position, the phone captures: Todd at the stove (center-left of frame), the fridge (far left), and a sliver of the main room behind him (right of frame)
- The phone's POV angle is **slightly below Todd's eye level** — looking up at him. This is the show's signature perspective

### The Stove
- Gas range, 4 burners. Old — came with the apartment. White enamel with chrome knobs, slightly yellowed with age. One burner has a temperamental igniter (clicks several times before lighting — an audio detail)
- The knobs have no markings left — the LOW/MED/HIGH text has worn off. This is why Todd doesn't know what medium heat is
- **The Post-it:** Not present in Eps 1-4. Appears in Ep 5 after Linda writes "MEDIUM HEAT" on it. From Ep 5 onward, it's stuck to the backsplash above the stove. Yellow Post-it, blue ink, Linda's handwriting (neat, all-caps, firefighter penmanship). It's there in every shot of the stove for the rest of the series. In the finale, Todd peels it off and holds it up to camera
- Backsplash: white subway tile, some grout discoloration. Clean but old

### The Sink
- Stainless steel single-basin, to the right of the stove
- **Window above the sink:** The apartment's only significant natural light source in the kitchen. Faces west — late afternoon light comes through warm and golden. No curtain, just a basic vinyl blind pulled halfway up. The window looks out onto... we never see. The light is the point, not the view
- A single dish sponge on the ledge. A bottle of generic dish soap
- This is where Todd eats in Ep 1 — standing at the sink, eating beans from the pan. The window light catches him in profile

### The Fridge
- Standard size, white, at the far end of the galley. Hums audibly (the apartment's ambient drone)
- **Interior (Ep 1):** Nearly empty. One can of beans. The furry cheese in plastic wrap. An amber bottle (hot sauce? Something unidentifiable). A box of baking soda that was there when he moved in. The light inside flickers — the bulb is dying
- **The fridge evolves:** By Ep 7 (post-grocery-trip), it has ranch, croutons, and canned goods. By Ep 11, real ingredients appear for the first time. The fridge interior is a barometer of Todd's progress
- **Exterior:** Nothing on the door. No magnets, no photos, no takeout menus. Bare white. (Linda's Post-it is on the stove, not the fridge — important distinction)

### The Junk Drawer
- Second drawer from the left, below the counter. The only drawer with anything in it
- **Contents (Ep 1):** The butter knife (Todd's primary tool), a hammer, loose batteries, a takeout menu he's never used, rubber bands, a pen that doesn't work, and miscellaneous detritus
- **Contents (Ep 2):** Same, plus the expensive watch — a gold dress watch, visible when the drawer opens. Todd reaches past it for the wooden spoon. By Ep 10, the watch is gone (sold in the Ep 7 purge)
- The drawer sticks slightly when opened — Todd has to tug it. A small character moment every time

### Cabinets
- Upper and lower, painted white, slightly chipped at the edges. Basic brass-tone knobs
- **Upper cabinets:** Mostly empty. One contains a few mismatched plates (3 dinner plates, 2 bowls — different patterns, clearly bought at a thrift store or came with the apartment). One contains a single box of salt
- **Lower cabinets:** A few pots and pans that look new and barely used. A hammer (lower left cabinet). A colander he doesn't know he has until approximately never

### The Knife Block
- Wooden, 5-slot. Sits on the counter near the stove
- 4 of 5 slots are full. The missing knife is the butter knife — it lives in the junk drawer, permanently repurposed. Todd has never used the actual kitchen knives. They're sharp and expensive-looking — possibly one of the few quality items in the kitchen (a gift? left by a previous tenant? never explained)
- In Ep 12, Linda pulls a chef's knife from the block and Todd reacts like he didn't know it was there

## THE MAIN ROOM

### The Futon
- Against the wall opposite the kitchen. Dark grey fabric, wooden frame. Perpetually in the "couch" position — Todd may not know it folds into a bed, or he prefers the couch configuration. A single pillow (no pillowcase, just the bare pillow). A folded blanket at one end — dark wool, expensive. One of his brought-from-home items
- No coffee table in front of it. Just floor space

### The Table
- Small, round, wooden. Room for two. Two mismatched chairs — one wooden (matches the table), one metal folding chair (doesn't match anything). Why does a man who lives alone have a table set for two? He doesn't think about it. We do
- The table sits between the kitchen and the futon, roughly centered in the room
- **In the finale:** This table becomes the dinner party set. Three plates, paper napkins, a single candle. The most effort Todd has ever put into a surface

### The Coat Rack
- Standing coat rack, dark wood, ornate — the kind of thing you'd find in a country estate foyer. It is wildly out of place in this apartment. Todd hung it by the door like it's normal. One coat hangs on it: a wool overcoat, dark charcoal, beautiful. He never wears it on the show
- This is one of the "expensive objects among cheap ones" details. It tells the story of what Todd packed when he left

### The Briefcase
- Brown leather, sitting on the floor against the wall near the coat rack. It's a professional attaché, the kind that costs more than the futon. It stays in the same spot all season
- **Ep 6:** Todd opens it to file the fire department bill inside. Camera catches: it's full of official-looking letters, envelopes, legal paper. He shuts it fast. It doesn't open again until the finale when Charlotte presents the trust document

### The Walls
- Off-white / cream. Bare. No art, no photos, no shelves, no hooks (except the coat rack). The walls are the loudest absence in the apartment — they say "I moved in and stopped"
- One wall has a small scuff mark at knee height. Existing damage, never addressed

### The Floor
- Hardwood, medium-dark. Original to the building. Some scratches and wear. A small area rug near the futon — cheap, geometric pattern, slightly crooked. It's the only decorative choice Todd has made, and it's not a good one

## LIGHTING

Lighting is mood. Lighting is story.

### Daytime (Most Episodes)
- **Primary source:** The window above the kitchen sink. West-facing, so afternoon/evening light is warm and golden. Morning light (if we ever see it) would be indirect and cool
- **Secondary source:** Overhead kitchen fluorescent. A single tube fixture, slightly yellowed. It casts a flat, institutional light with a faint green tint. When both the window and the fluorescent are on, the kitchen has a push-pull between warm natural light and cold artificial light. The warmth wins, but the fluorescent is always there
- **Main room:** No overhead light. A single floor lamp near the futon — warm bulb, dim. The main room is always slightly darker than the kitchen. Todd lives in the kitchen

### Evening / Night
- The fluorescent becomes the dominant source. The window goes dark (or shows city light — faint, ambient). The apartment gets smaller at night — the warm amber contracts, the shadows deepen. Evening scenes should feel more intimate, more enclosed
- Todd doesn't own candles (until the finale, when he buys one for the dinner party)

### The Phone Screen
- When we see the Instagram interface or Todd's phone screen, it's a cool blue-white light in the warm apartment. A small, modern rectangle in an analog space

## SOUND ENVIRONMENT

The apartment has a voice. These ambient sounds should be present in every scene:

- **The fridge hum:** Low, constant. The apartment's heartbeat. Louder in quiet moments
- **Occasional traffic:** Muffled through walls. Cars passing, never close. The apartment is not on a busy street but not on a dead one
- **A neighbor's TV:** Faint, indistinct, through the wall to the left of the kitchen. Only audible in very quiet moments. We can never make out what's on. It's just a reminder that other people exist
- **The stove igniter:** Click-click-click-WHOMP when Todd lights a burner. The delayed ignition is a micro-tension moment every time
- **Pipes:** Occasional knocking when water runs. Old building
- **The baseboard gap (Ep 8+):** After the baseball episode, attentive listeners might hear faint scratching from the wall. The rat

## GENERATION NOTES

**The kitchen is the consistency anchor for the entire series.** Every element in this document — the counter, the stove, the fridge, the window, the fluorescent light — must remain stable across all generated shots. The kitchen is the "set" in the theatrical sense: it doesn't change, so when small things do change (the Post-it appearing, the watch disappearing), the audience notices.

**Key spatial relationship:** Phone on counter (screen left) → Todd at stove (center) → fridge (screen right) → sliver of main room (far right). This composition is the show's visual home base. Deviations from it (Todd at the sink, Todd at the table) should feel like we're seeing a different part of a consistent space.

**Prompt fragment (kitchen, standard angle):**
> Small galley kitchen in a studio apartment, laminate countertop, white subway tile backsplash, gas stove with worn knobs, stainless steel sink beneath a window with warm afternoon light, white fridge at far end, overhead fluorescent tube light, sparse and clean but old, warm amber palette with slight green-tint from fluorescent, semi-stylized painterly realism
