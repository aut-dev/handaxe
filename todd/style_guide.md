# A Day in the Life of Todd — Tone & Style Guide

## The Core Idea

This is a show about sincerity in a world that rewards performance. Todd is not in on the joke. He's not cringe — he's earnest. The visual language has to honor that. Everything should feel warm, close, a little lonely, and completely un-ironic.

---

## Visual Style: "Warm Realism"

**Not photorealistic. Not cartoon. Somewhere honest.**

The look is **semi-stylized realism** — think the grounded end of studio animation. Characters have real proportions, real weight, real skin texture, but the world has a slight painterly softness to it. Edges aren't razor-sharp. Light blooms a little more than it should. It feels like a memory of a real apartment, not a photograph of one.

This serves two purposes:
1. It gives us room to maintain character consistency across AI-generated shots (photorealism is the hardest to keep coherent).
2. It matches the tone — Todd's world is slightly heightened, slightly warmer than reality, because we're seeing it through his eyes.

**Reference touchstones:**
- The domestic warmth of *Ratatouille* (kitchen scenes, food close-ups)
- The textured loneliness of *Her* (apartment interiors, soft light, single figure in frame)
- The lo-fi intimacy of *Marcel the Shell* (found-footage framing, small world)
- The naturalistic palette of *Eighth Grade* (unflattering but empathetic)

---

## Color Palette

### Todd's Apartment
- **Dominant:** Warm amber, soft cream, muted mustard
- **Accents:** Olive green (the walls), faded terracotta (a towel, a mug)
- **Shadows:** Warm brown, never cold blue or grey
- **Kitchen light:** Overhead fluorescent that casts a slight yellow-green — the one "ugly" light source, but it becomes familiar and eventually comforting

### Food
- Food should look exactly as appetizing (or horrifying) as it actually is. Scorched beans are dark, crusty, sad. The Episode 12 roast chicken should glow. Food is the show's emotional barometer — light and color it honestly.

### Fire Department / Exterior
- Cooler palette when the outside world intrudes. Linda's uniform is standard navy/yellow. The hallway outside Todd's door is fluorescent-white, institutional. The contrast with Todd's warm amber cave should be noticeable.

### Instagram UI Overlay
- When we see Todd's phone screen / view counts, use the actual Instagram UI aesthetic — clean white, familiar iconography. The view count numbers should be small, unobtrusive, almost easy to miss. They're not a joke we're pointing at. They're a detail for the attentive viewer.

---

## Camera Philosophy

This is a show shot on **Todd's phone**. That's not just a plot device — it's the entire visual grammar.

### Primary Camera: Todd's Phone
- **Position:** Propped on the kitchen counter, slightly below eye level, or held one-handed
- **Angle:** Slightly off-center, slightly too low or too high — the way a phone propped against a cereal box actually looks
- **Movement:** Minimal. Occasionally Todd adjusts it (we see the frame lurch). When he picks it up, it's handheld and a little shaky
- **Framing:** Todd is often slightly off-center or partially cut off. He doesn't know how to frame a shot. Sometimes his forehead is cropped. Sometimes there's too much counter in frame
- **Focus:** Shallow when handheld (phone autofocus behavior — sometimes hunting, sometimes locking on the wrong thing)

### The "Invisible" Camera (Reveal Crumbs)
- Certain moments are caught by the phone when Todd isn't paying attention — the watch in the junk drawer, the Dad call, the signed baseball. These use the **same camera** (it's still his phone) but the framing shifts: Todd moves out of the shot, and the phone catches what he didn't mean to show
- These moments should feel accidental, not directed. No dramatic push-in. No music sting. The phone just happens to be pointing the right way

### What We Never Do
- No crane shots, no dolly moves, no rack focus, no split screens
- No shot-reverse-shot (there's only one camera)
- No establishing exteriors (we never see the building from outside — we're trapped in Todd's world the way he is)
- No slow motion
- No letterboxing — this is vertical or near-square, phone aspect ratio

---

## Aspect Ratio

**9:16 (vertical) for the Instagram content. 4:5 for the "full episode" frame.**

The show lives in the tension between these two frames:
- When Todd is filming for Instagram, we're in **9:16** — tight, vertical, phone-native
- The "episode" frame is **4:5** — slightly wider, showing a bit more of the apartment around the phone's POV, giving us room for the things Todd doesn't see

In practice: the 4:5 frame contains the 9:16 phone footage, with the edges of the apartment visible around it. Think of it as: we're watching Todd's phone, but we can also see just past the edges of what he's recording.

## Sound Design

### Voice
- Todd's voice is the show. Warm, mid-range, unhurried. He speaks like a cooking show host who learned everything from watching cooking shows. Confident cadence, zero expertise. He never rushes. He pauses for emphasis on the wrong words
- When he's alone and not "performing," his voice drops — quieter, less projected, more real. This shift becomes more frequent as the season progresses

### Ambient Sound
- The apartment has a sound: fridge hum, occasional traffic, a neighbor's TV through the wall (muffled, indistinct). These should be present in every scene — the apartment is alive
- Cooking sounds are prominent and specific: the scrape of the butter knife on the can, the hiss of beans hitting a hot pan, the clang of the hammer. These are almost ASMR-level close

### Music
- **Almost none.** Todd sometimes hums — a few bars of something, then stops, like he forgot the rest or got self-conscious. That's it
- No score. No background music. No needle drops. The absence of music is a choice — it keeps us in Todd's real time, with no emotional cues telling us how to feel
- **Exception:** The season finale post-credits could use a single, simple piece — a quiet guitar or piano line. Earned through 12 episodes of silence

### Sound Effects
- Naturalistic only. Real sounds, real space. The smoke alarm is harsh and loud. The fire truck outside is muffled through walls. The phone buzzing on the counter is specific and recognizable
- The one heightened sound: the **view count ticking up** in the finale. A tiny, almost subliminal digital chirp each time the number increments. Subtle enough that you might not consciously hear it, but it builds

## Editing & Pacing

### Rhythm
- Slow by internet standards. Scenes breathe. Todd takes his time. The comedy comes from duration — he holds up the furry cheese for a beat too long. He stares at the empty sink where the pasta was for an uncomfortably long time. Don't cut away from the awkwardness
- **No jump cuts.** This is a single continuous take from the phone's perspective. If Todd walks off-camera, we wait for him to come back. The camera doesn't follow

### Transitions
- **Hard cut to black** between scenes. No fades, no wipes, no transitions. Black, then the next shot. Like separate Instagram posts
- Episode title cards: simple white text on black. The episode title. Nothing else

### Episode Length
- **3-4 minutes** for Episodes 1-10 (single-concept cooking episodes)
- **5-7 minutes** for Episodes 11-13 (the dinner party arc earns more time)
- Episode 1 target: **3 minutes**

## Character Design Notes (Episode 1 Relevant)

### Todd
- **Age:** Early 30s
- **Build:** Average, slightly soft — not athletic, not overweight. A body that's never done manual labor
- **Face:** Open, expressive without being exaggerated. He has a face that's easy to trust. Slight stubble, not groomed, not scruffy — just someone who doesn't think about it
- **Hair:** Medium brown, a little too long, no particular style. He pushes it out of his eyes sometimes
- **Wardrobe:** The disconnect is key. He wears obviously expensive clothes badly — a cashmere sweater with food stains, tailored pants rolled up wrong, the $800 Italian loafers in the kitchen. He doesn't dress down to match his apartment; he just wears what he has. Over the season, the clothes get more worn, more stained. He never buys new ones
- **Episode 1 outfit:** Navy cashmere crewneck (slightly too big), grey wool trousers, loafers. No apron. No awareness that you'd want one

### The Apartment
- **Size:** Studio. One room plus a bathroom we never see. Kitchen along one wall, futon against another, a small table with two chairs (why two? he lives alone)
- **State:** Sparse but not bare. A few expensive objects among cheap ones — the leather briefcase, the coat rack, the Italian loafers by the door. Everything else is minimal: bare walls, no art, basic bedding. It looks like someone moved in three months ago and never decorated. Because he did
- **The Kitchen:** Small galley-style. Limited counter space. Old appliances (came with the apartment). A few pots and pans that look brand new (bought but barely used). A knife block with one knife missing (it's the butter knife, always in use elsewhere). The junk drawer. The stove with no Post-it yet (that comes in Episode 5)
- **The Fridge:** Nearly empty. The furry cheese. A can of beans. A bottle of something unidentifiable. The light inside flickers

## What This Show Is NOT

- Not a satire of influencer culture (Todd is too sincere for satire)
- Not a cringe comedy (we are always with Todd, never above him)
- Not a mystery show (the reveal crumbs are texture, not puzzle pieces)
- Not aspirational (the food is bad, the apartment is bare, the view counts are low)
- Not depressing (Todd is happy. He chose this. The show respects that choice)

The tone is: **you are watching someone learn to live, one terrible meal at a time, and it's funnier and sadder and more hopeful than it has any right to be.**
